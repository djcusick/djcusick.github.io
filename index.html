David's Research at the University of Washington

My current research in the Seidler Lab involves the study of X-ray Absorption Spectroscopy (XAS). XAS is a measurement used to understand the molecular structure of materials. It works like this: a beam of x-rays is aimed at a sample material; some x-rays penetrate the sample while others are absorbed into it. The laws of quantum mechanics predict that the number of penetrating x-rays divided by the number of x-rays in the original beam (denoted IT/I0IT/I0) depends sensitively on both the energy of the incident x-ray beam and the sample's molecular structure. By measuring ITIT and I0I0 at many different beam energies for a particular sample, detailed information about the sample's molecular structure can be obtained and encoded in a plot of −log(IT/I0)−log(IT/I0) versus beam energy.

My personal research goal is to produce a computer simulation that characterizes three systematic errors in XAS measurements. Without getting into the nitty-gritty details, the first systematic error occurs when a percentage of x-rays misses the sample entirely by going through holes in the sample; this percentage is denoted by alpha. The second error has to do with unexpected abberations in the sample thickness; the severity of those abberations is characterized by the parameter labeled thickness factor. The third error occurs when the x-rays corresponding to one beam energy accidentally influence the value of the measurement at a different beam energy; the parameter labeled Lorentzian FWHM indicates the severity of this effect. It turns out that all three of these systematic errors can influence XAS measurements to some degree. Therefore I hope that my simulation will be of benefit to many XAS scientists, who can use it for troubleshooting to find out which systematic errors could be influencing their data.

Check out the current simulation version here, which plots −log(IT/I0)−log(IT/I0) versus beam energy. I made this simulation using a programming language called JavaScript. The red line is actual experimental data taken from a sample of V2O5V2O5 (a chemical compound called vanadium oxide) while the yellow line is what this data would look like if there had been systematic errors in the measurement. I recommend keeping the Normalize checkbox checked to see a fairer comparison between the red and yellow lines. Also, use the Convolve with Lorentzian checkbox to implement the third systematic error (otherwise the Lorentzian FWHM slider will do nothing). By default (and with Normalize enabled) the sliders are initialized in a configuration for which no errors are present. But you can see that as the slider values are changed, these systematic errors can significantly distort the data from how it really should appear.
